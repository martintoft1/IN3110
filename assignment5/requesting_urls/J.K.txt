<p><b>Hans Rosling</b> (<small>Swedish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Swedish" title="Help:IPA/Swedish">[ˈhɑːns ˈrûːslɪŋ]</a></span>; 27 July 1948 – 7 February 2017) was a Swedish physician, academic, and public speaker. He was a professor of international health at <a href="/wiki/Karolinska_Institute" title="Karolinska Institute">Karolinska Institute</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and was the co-founder and chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Gapminder_Foundation" title="Gapminder Foundation">Gapminder Foundation</a>, which developed the <a href="/wiki/Trendalyzer" title="Trendalyzer">Trendalyzer</a> software system. He held presentations around the world, including several <a href="/wiki/Ted_Talk" class="mw-redirect" title="Ted Talk">TED Talks</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> in which he promoted the use of data (and <a href="/wiki/Data_visualization" title="Data visualization">data visualization</a>) to explore <a href="/wiki/Development_studies" title="Development studies">development</a> issues.<sup id="cite_ref-gapminder_death_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gapminder_death-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His posthumously published book <i><a href="/wiki/Factfulness:_Ten_Reasons_We%27re_Wrong_About_the_World_%E2%80%93_and_Why_Things_Are_Better_Than_You_Think" title="Factfulness: Ten Reasons We&#39;re Wrong About the World – and Why Things Are Better Than You Think">Factfulness</a></i>, coauthored with <a href="/wiki/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund" title="Anna Rosling Rönnlund">Anna Rosling Rönnlund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ola_Rosling" title="Ola Rosling">Ola Rosling</a>, became an international bestseller.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rosling was born in <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a>, Sweden, on 28 July 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> From 1967 to 1974, he studied statistics and medicine at <a href="/wiki/Uppsala_University" title="Uppsala University">Uppsala University</a>, and in 1972 he studied public health at <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Medical_College" title="St. John&#39;s Medical College">St. John's Medical College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. He became a licensed physician in 1976 and from 1979 to 1981 he served as District Medical Officer in <a href="/wiki/Nacala" title="Nacala">Nacala</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>. In 1981, he began investigating an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Konzo" title="Konzo">konzo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Paralytic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paralytic">paralytic</a> disease first described in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rosling_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosling-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> His investigations earned him a <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> at Uppsala University in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosling_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosling-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Rosling was <a href="/wiki/Dyslexia" title="Dyslexia">dyslexic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosling presented the television documentary <i>The Joy of Stats</i>, which was broadcast in the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">BBC Four</a> in December 2010 and has been made available to 'catch up' on BBC iPlayer since.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He presented a documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Panic_%E2%80%94_The_Truth_about_Population" title="Don&#39;t Panic — The Truth about Population">Don't Panic — The Truth about Population</a></i> for the <i><a href="/wiki/This_World_(TV_series)" title="This World (TV series)">This World</a></i> series using a <a href="/wiki/Musion_Eyeliner" title="Musion Eyeliner">Musion 3D</a> projection display,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> which appeared on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> in the UK in November 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2015 he presented the documentary <i>Don't Panic: How to End Poverty in 15 Years</i>, which was produced by Wingspan and aired on the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> just ahead of the announcement of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN's</a> <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosling was a <a href="/wiki/Sword_swallower" class="mw-redirect" title="Sword swallower">sword swallower</a>, as demonstrated in the final moments of his second talk at the <a href="/wiki/TED_(conference)" title="TED (conference)">TED conference</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2009 he was listed as one of 100 leading global thinkers by <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy" title="Foreign Policy">Foreign Policy</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and in 2011 as one of 100 most creative people in business by <i><a href="/wiki/Fast_Company_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast Company (magazine)">Fast Company</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 2011 he was elected member of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Academy_of_Engineering_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish Academy of Engineering Sciences">Swedish Academy of Engineering Sciences</a> and in 2012 as member of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish Academy of Sciences">Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> He was included in the <a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100"><i>Time</i> 100</a> list of the world's 100 most influential people in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_obit_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_obit-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_in_healthcare">Work in healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work in healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rosling spent two decades studying outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Konzo" title="Konzo">konzo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralytic disease</a>, in remote rural areas across Africa and supervised more than ten PhD students.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> His work with Julie Cliff, Johannes Mårtensson, Per Lundqvist, and Bo Sörbo found that outbreaks occur among hunger-stricken rural populations in Africa where a diet dominated by insufficiently processed <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> results in simultaneous malnutrition and high dietary <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> intake.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosling_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosling-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbc-2019_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2019-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosling's research also concerned other links between economic development, agriculture, poverty and health.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He was a health adviser to the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a> and several aid agencies. In 1993 he was one of the initiators of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="Médecins Sans Frontières">Médecins Sans Frontières</a> in Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> At Karolinska Institutet he was head of the Division of International Health (IHCAR) from 2001 to 2007. As chairman of the Karolinska International Research and Training Committee (1998–2004), he started health research collaborations with universities in Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America. He started new courses on global health and co-authored a textbook on global health that promotes a fact-based worldview.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trendalyzer_and_Gapminder">Trendalyzer and Gapminder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trendalyzer and Gapminder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund,_Hans_Rosling,_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg/220px-Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg/330px-Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg/440px-Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund%2C_Hans_Rosling%2C_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2856" data-file-height="1439" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund,_Hans_Rosling,_and_Ola_Rosling_on_%22Factfulness%22.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>(Left to right) <a href="/wiki/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund" title="Anna Rosling Rönnlund">Anna Rosling Rönnlund</a>, Hans Rosling, and <a href="/wiki/Ola_Rosling" title="Ola Rosling">Ola Rosling</a> discuss their book <i>Factfulness</i> in 2016.</div></div></div>
<p>Rosling's son, <a href="/wiki/Ola_Rosling" title="Ola Rosling">Ola Rosling</a>, built the <a href="/wiki/Trendalyzer" title="Trendalyzer">Trendalyzer</a> software to animate data compiled by the UN and the World Bank that helped him explain the world with graphics.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Rosling co-founded the Gapminder Foundation together with his son Ola and daughter-in-law <a href="/wiki/Anna_Rosling_R%C3%B6nnlund" title="Anna Rosling Rönnlund">Anna Rosling Rönnlund</a> to develop Trendalyzer to convert international statistics into moving, interactive graphics. The provocative presentations that have resulted have made him famous,<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and his lectures using Gapminder graphics to visualize world development have won awards.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The interactive animations are freely available from the Foundation's website.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When he was 20, in 1968, doctors told Rosling that there was something wrong with his liver and as a consequence, he stopped drinking <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a>. Aged 29, with a young family, he had testicular cancer which was successfully treated. In 1989, he was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C" title="Hepatitis C">hepatitis C</a>. Over the years this progressed and he developed <a href="/wiki/Liver_cirrhosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Liver cirrhosis">liver cirrhosis</a>. At the beginning of 2013, he was in the early stages of liver failure. However, at the same time, new hepatitis C drugs were released and he went to Japan to buy the drugs needed to treat the infection. He expressed concerns in the media over the restricted use of the new drugs due to high costs, stating that it is a crime not to give every person with hepatitis C access to the drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosling was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a> in 2016, and died of the disease on 7 February 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-gapminder_death_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gapminder_death-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Reception_of_Rosling.27s_views"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_of_Rosling's_views">Reception of Rosling's views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception of Rosling&#039;s views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rosling is commonly described as an optimist, though he personally rejected the label. In his posthumous book <i><a href="/wiki/Factfulness:_Ten_Reasons_We%27re_Wrong_About_the_World_%E2%80%93_and_Why_Things_Are_Better_Than_You_Think" title="Factfulness: Ten Reasons We&#39;re Wrong About the World – and Why Things Are Better Than You Think">Factfulness: Ten Reasons We're Wrong About the World – and Why Things Are Better Than You Think</a></i> he wrote "The five global risks that concern me most are the risks of a global pandemic, financial collapse, world war, climate change, and extreme poverty.”<sup id="cite_ref-:0_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In summary, he wrote, "People often call me an optimist, because I show them the enormous progress they didn't know about. That makes me angry. I'm not an optimist. That makes me sound naive. I'm a very serious “possibilist”. That's something I made up. It means someone who neither hopes without reason, nor fears without reason, someone who constantly resists the overdramatic worldview. As a possibilist, I see all this progress, and it fills me with conviction and hope that further progress is possible. This is not optimistic. It is having a clear and reasonable idea about how things are. It is having a worldview that is constructive and useful.”<sup id="cite_ref-:0_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his later years he advocated on behalf of refugees from Syria and partnered with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Refugees" title="United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees">United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees</a> (UNHCR) in this effort.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In his last book he wrote repeatedly about the tragedy of the war in Syria saying: "The Syrian conflict will most likely prove to be the deadliest in the world since the Ethiopian-Eritrean war of 1998 to 2000.”<sup id="cite_ref-:0_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, some experts consider Rosling's world-view excessively rose-tinted, or anti-environmental. For instance, in <i>The One-Sided Worldview of Hans Rosling</i><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Christian Berggren, a Swedish professor of industrial management, argues that <i>Factfulness</i>, "presents a highly biased sample of statistics as the true perspective on global development, avoids analysis of negative trends, and refrains from discussing difficult issues". Seeing Rosling as more optimist than "possibilist", Berggren remarks that, "<i>Factfulness</i> includes many graphs of 'bad things in decline' and 'good things on the rise' but not a single graph of 'bad things on the rise'." In 2013 in <i>The Ecologist</i> Robin Maynard reported Rosling as raging against the UN's population projections, and against some ecological objections to development: "I don't give a damn about polar bears! I can live without polar bears."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hence, Rosling has been criticized as being <a href="/wiki/Pollyannaism" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollyannaism">Pollyannaist</a> about the global political situation in the face of tragedies such as the long-running conflict in Syria, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His work on population growth has also been criticized by <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>, the U.S. biologist and Professor of Population Studies at Stanford University, and <a href="/wiki/Anne_H._Ehrlich" title="Anne H. Ehrlich">Anne H. Ehrlich</a>, associate director of the Center for Conservation Biology at Stanford University, in an article, published online by the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Alliance_for_Humanity_and_the_Biosphere" title="Millennium Alliance for Humanity and the Biosphere">MAHB</a>, titled "A Confused Statistician." The Ehrlichs also warn that, while some trends that Rosling cites may indeed be positive, there is the possibility of total collapse of those trends if social and political instabilities occur.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
